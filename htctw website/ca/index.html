Electrolane

Group1

For How To Change The World project, our team members from different engineering fields introduce a concept of electrolane to tackle the problem of air and noise pollution in M25.

About this project


The M25 is one of the busiest roads in the UK. Every day, more than 200,000 vehicles use this ring road. [1] The annual NOx concentration on this road is well above the regulatory limit set by the UK Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs. The proximity of the M25 to large residential communities also exacerbate the health consequences of roadside NOx pollution. Studies have shown that high concentrations of NOx increase the risk of reduced fetal growth by 6%, as well as hypertension and cardiovascular diseases by 1.5% each. [2,3] NOx-related health issues cost the UK over £6 billion each year. [4] Electric vehicles (EVs) produce zero NOx emissions and are extremely quiet. It is estimated that there will be 36 million EVs on the road in the UK by 2040. [5] The uptake of EVs will be of significant benefit to the NOx pollution on congested urban roads such as the M25. To facilitate the uptake of EVs, we propose to electrify the outer lane on sections of the M25 passing through populated communities. This will reduce roadside NOx pollution overtime and mitigate the air and noise impacts on these resident groups.

Roads in 37 out of 43 zones in the UK exceed annual average NOx concentration of 40μg/m3 [6]

High NOx concentration increases probability of health problems

  • Risk of reduced fetal growth increased by 6%
  • Risk of hypertension increased by 1.5%
  • Risk of cardiovascular diseases increased by 1.5%

Solution


We are proposing a roadside barrier which wirelessly charges electric/hybrid vehicles travelling on it. Electric current will be passed through coils in the barrier, creating a magnetic field. Cars can then be wirelessly charged via a receiver on the car, through electromagnetic induction [1]. The outer portion of the barrier will be concrete as this is known to be the safest, and the inner wiring will be copper, due to its low price and high conductivity.And at roughly 60 km/h, for 2 km, at 200 kW, 1/6th of a Nissan Leaf Battery can be charged. Currently there is significant range anxiety associated with EVs, due to long charge times and lack of charging points. By providing dynamic charging during travel, our solution reduces this anxiety, and incentives electric cars [2]. As electric cars have zero NOx emissions this will directly better the air quality on the M25. This solution is highly advanced from a mechanical engineering perspective. Current solutions like electric rails in Sweden, use mechanical arms to connect cars to an electric rail. This solution leads to severe erosion and wear on both the rail and the mechanical arm, making it less sustainable[4]. Our solution is also highly advanced from a business perspective as construction/maintenance of the solution are economical. This is because the electrified barriers will be on the side of the road, and thus they will require minimum to no lanes to be closed.In comparison, similar solutions which provide wireless charging within the roads, would require one or more lanes to be closed. Obviously closed lanes lead to excess congestion. And given that even short construction projects of 3-7 years, lead to up to 7% increases in traffic, decreases in congestion are extremely valuable. This is because, excessive traffic means more idle time, and less economic productivity[6]. We also analysed the solution from a chemical engineering and computer science perspective. Using chemical engineering principles of flow to analyse traffic flow data, and computer scientists’ data analysis skills we were able to determine that the highest NOx levels are on sections of the M25 in kent[3]. Thus we have decided to focus our solution on this section .

Design Sustainability


    The product itself:

  1. Reduces the need for using ICEs and hence non-renewable energy sources
  2. Reduces the amount of construction required to implement
  3. Reduces areas required to physically charge vehicles
  4. Can make use of the barrier infrastructure already built in place
  5. Recyclable (concrete)
  6. Easy to replace sections due to modular design of concrete barriers
  7. Corrosion and fire resistant
  8. Only requires one lane for construction, allowing for continual traffic flow which has monetary benefits for the client

    The environment:

  1. Reduces toxic air emissions, making it safer for residents around M25
  2. Less environmental strain due to less construction required
  3. Can replace some steel barriers already in place to improve driver safety of M25
  4. Lower carbon footprint than steel
  5. Requires less land work to hold in place

The design itself of the Electrolane makes it easily sustainable as a product, since all it requires is a power supply so that it can charge the vehicles via conduction as they drive along a portion of the M25. Being made primarily out of concrete makes it not only extremely tough and durable, but also completely recyclable and reusable for other purposes such as asphalt paving; also notable to mention that this would mean less concrete is simply dumped at a landfill as waste. Replacing barriers would be significantly easier than resurfacing roads to update or repair the electrical infrastructure, as well as being less demanding in terms of construction, since the barriers are manufactured as modules rather than long lengths. Furthermore, only a single lane would need to be closed off, meaning that there is still traffic flow in the remaining lanes, overall having monetary benefits for the client due to saved resources and time. All the above points make this product a highly resilient, The Electrolane also has multiple benefits for the environment, one of which being the intended reduction of toxic air pollutants such as NOx, whose levels can be as high as 80μg/m3(twice that of the regulatory annual mean) on certain portions of the M25. Additionally, the carbon footprint of concrete is around 20% that of the same volume steel, so the manufacturing methods of these barriers are also relatively clean. The positive implications that this has for the residents living around such polluted parts is quite large, and as a result, hospitals would benefit from having to deal with less patients who suffer from lung related conditions such as asthma.

Risks/Constrains


Creating this solution will further promote the UNDP's sustainability goals, specifically targeting #9 regarding industry, innovation and infrastructure. Even though this projects’ potential outcome can be seen from a very beneficial and desirable perspective there are still some issues that need to be brought forward. The most fundamental of it would be the actual economical gain that would occur if this project would be undertaken. As Electrolane will be offering the solution free of charge for motorists on the highway, then there has to be some capital loss for the project sponsor. This goes against one of the principles related to this sustainability goal, namely providing feasible economical solutions. Therefore, this brings out the question if there are other measures to tackle the #9 UNDP’s goals while also capitalizing on the solution. The team has considered this to be the current necessary solution, even though its constraints regarding capital gain. This is because as the solution directly relates to an investment in infrastructure that promotes the use of renewable energy. The other assumption regarding the introduction of Electrolane is that it will act as an incentive for motorists to consider electric vehicles. This relies on the idea that consumers will now be incentivised to purchase an electric vehicle as one of the key issues of range is resolved [1]. However, this is a risk of the project as the incentive is not in terms of direct monetary gain or improved social wellbeing, but only a more habitable environment for electric vehicle motorists. Relating this to the UNDP's #9 sustainability goal it involves supporting human well-being by affordable access, as Electrolane will be free of charge for motorists. Creating a more habitable environment for electric vehicles, promoting reduced NOx levels with minimum societal disruption is Electrolanes mean of measure of supporting human well-being. Electrolane works by initializing a magnetic field between the barrier and the electric vehicle, allowing electrical induction. Considering the by-products that occur when using Electrolane, there is a questionable health hazard that has to be considered. Magnetic fields are currently considered by the World Health Organisation [2] to be not harmful for humans if there is a low exposure level. However, our understanding on the impacts of electro-magnatic radiation on human health is still not complete [3].
